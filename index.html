The Efficiency Paradox: Bridging the Description-Experience Gap

The inspiration for my video paper, "The Efficiency Paradox," originated from a series of high-friction interactions with Carousell’s "Smart Listing" feature. As an active user of the platform, I observed a recurring pattern: the AI-driven automation, designed to save time, often created more work for the user. Two specific events served as my primary sources of evidence. First, an experiment where I uploaded a car photo that the system misidentified as "Property," and second, a pricing experiment where the AI suggested 960 HKD for a watch that eventually sold for 500 HKD. These were not merely technical glitches; they were fundamental violations of the Match between the System and the Real World.

In my video paper, I justify the critique using the Gulf of Execution and the Description-Experience Gap. While Carousell successfully bridges the Gulf of Execution by reducing the number of steps required to list an item, it fails to support the user’s Gulf of Evaluation. When the system provides an inaccurate price or category, the user is left with a "Verification Burden." They must now cross-reference the AI's logic with their own market knowledge, effectively canceling out the time saved by the initial automation.

After taking this course, my perspective has evolved toward Human-Centric Augmentation. I now understand that true efficiency is not about removing the human from the process, but about supporting User Agency. The course taught me that "Smart" features often lack Transparency. If a user doesn't understand why an AI suggested a specific price, they cannot trust the system. This realization led to my final recommendations in the video paper: shifting from a "Black Box" model to a "Glass Box" model using Confidence-Based Transparency and Explainable AI.

This paradigm shift has directly changed how I work as a CRM Email Specialist at Crypto.com. Before this course, when I was coding emails and in-app messages, my main goal was "technical deliverability"—basically just making sure the message arrived and looked correct. I treated these messages as a one-way broadcast: I sent information, and the user received it.

Now, thanks to the HCI principles I’ve learned, I view every message as an interface that requires careful design. I focus on reducing Cognitive Load; instead of cluttering an email with too much information, I now design layouts that are easier to scan. My goal is to make the "next step" obvious so the user doesn't have to feel overwhelmed by too many choices.

I also work on Bridging the Gulf of Evaluation. In the past, if a user received a notification about a transaction, they might feel confused about what happened next. Now, I ensure the message clearly explains the "System Status." If they see an in-app message, they should instantly understand what has been processed and what they need to do. I want to close the gap between what the computer is doing and what the user actually understands.

Ultimately, I now advocate for Empowerment over Noise. This course has taught me that a well-coded email is more than just clean HTML; it’s a tool to build trust. By respecting the user's time and mental effort, I ensure our communications help them reach their goals—like successfully completing a trade—rather than just adding digital noise to their day.
